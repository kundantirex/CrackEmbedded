Bluetooth Low Energy, explained one layer at a time.

You've never touched Bluetooth — that's fine. This walks every interview topic bottom-up in plain language, with one worked example and a block diagram per idea, then hands you projects you can build on an i.MX93 FRDM board or a Raspberry Pi.

Read it in order. Each topic builds on the one before it. The recurring blue strip below is the 2.4 GHz band — the single picture that ties the whole radio together. Learn to read it and half of BLE clicks into place.

Part 1

Fundamentals

What Bluetooth actually is, and the radio it lives on.

01 Bluetooth Classic vs BLE

Bluetooth is two different technologies sharing one brand. Bluetooth Classic (BR/EDR) is built for continuous streams — audio, file transfer. Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) is built for tiny bursts of data with the radio asleep most of the time, so a coin cell can last years.

 Bluetooth Classic (BR/EDR)BLE (Low Energy)
Made forStreaming: headphones, car audio, file transferShort bursts: sensors, beacons, control
Channels79 × 1 MHz40 × 2 MHz
Connection setupSlow (~100 ms), heavyFast (~3 ms), light
PowerHigh (always on during link)Very low (radio sleeps between events)
TopologyPiconet (1 master, up to 7 slaves)Star, plus broadcast & mesh
Data modelProfiles over RFCOMM/L2CAPGATT (attributes)
Example

Your wireless earbuds use Classic (constant audio). Your fitness band uses BLE — it wakes up, spits out a heart-rate number, and sleeps again.

Say this in the interview

"BLE trades throughput for power. It's not 'slow Bluetooth' — it's a different radio profile optimized for devices that sleep 99% of the time." Nearly all modern IoT/firmware work is BLE, not Classic.

02 The BLE protocol stack

BLE is a layered stack split into two halves: the Controller (the radio + timing, usually hard real-time hardware) and the Host (the logic your app talks to). The HCI is the standard wire between them.

  • PHY — the actual radio: modulates bits onto 2.4 GHz.
  • Link Layer — timing, advertising, connections, retransmission. Hard real-time.
  • HCI — standard command/event interface between the two halves.
  • L2CAP — packet multiplexing / fragmentation.
  • ATT / GATT — how data is organized and exchanged.
  • SMP — pairing, keys, encryption.
  • GAP — roles & connection procedures (who advertises, who scans).
Firmware angle

As a firmware engineer you mostly live in GAP + GATT (your app) sitting on a vendor stack that already implements the Controller. If the job is "controller firmware," you go deeper into Link Layer timing and PHY.

03 PHY: 1M, 2M, and Coded (long range)

The PHY is how bits are put on the air. BLE has three flavors, and picking the right one is a real design trade-off between speed, range, and power.

PHYSymbol rateTrade-offUse it for
LE 1M1 Msym/sThe original defaultEverything, baseline
LE 2M2 Msym/s2× faster, shorter range, less time on air = less power/txHigh throughput, OTA updates
LE Coded (S=2/S=8)500 / 125 kbps~4× the range, much slowerLong-range sensors, whole-building reach
Example

Pushing a 200 KB firmware image? Switch to 2M PHY — half the airtime, faster update, less battery per transfer. A door sensor across a warehouse? Coded PHY so the link survives the distance.

Why "Coded" reaches farther

It repeats/encodes each bit (forward error correction). The receiver can rebuild the message from a weaker, noisier signal — trading data rate for sensitivity. 2M and Coded arrived with Bluetooth 5.0.

04 The 2.4 GHz band & Adaptive Frequency Hopping

BLE lives in the crowded 2.4 GHz ISM band — the same space as Wi-Fi, microwaves, Zigbee. Its survival trick is hopping: a connection constantly jumps between channels so no single busy channel kills it.

  • Frequency hopping — each connection event uses a new channel, picked by a hop algorithm both sides agree on.
  • AFH (Adaptive) — the device measures which channels are failing (Wi-Fi collisions) and drops them from the hop map, so it stops using the bad ones.
Example

Your office Wi-Fi hammers channels near 2437 MHz. AFH notices packets there keep failing and quietly removes those BLE channels from rotation — the connection stays alive using the clean ones.

05 Advertising channels vs data channels

Of the 40 channels, BLE reserves 3 for advertising (37, 38, 39) and uses the other 37 for data inside a connection. The 3 advertising channels are deliberately spread across the band so at least one usually dodges Wi-Fi.

24023 advertising channels are spaced to avoid the 3 main Wi-Fi blocks2480
  • Advertising channels (37, 38, 39) — used before a connection: broadcasting presence, being discovered. Placed at 2402, 2426, 2480 MHz.
  • Data channels (0–36) — used inside a connection, with hopping + AFH.
Why this matters

Wi-Fi occupies 3 big chunks of the band. The 3 advertising channels sit in the gaps between them — so even in a noisy office, a phone can still discover your device on at least one.

Part 2

Link Layer

The state machine that runs advertising, scanning, and every connection.

06 Link Layer states

The Link Layer is a state machine. At any moment the radio is in exactly one of five states.

  • Standby — idle, radio off. Default low-power state.
  • Advertising — broadcasting packets so others can find/connect. (Peripheral)
  • Scanning — listening for advertisers. (Central)
  • Initiating — heard an advertiser, sending a connection request.
  • Connection — linked; exchanging data with hopping.

07 Advertising types

An advertisement isn't one thing — you choose whether devices can connect, whether they can ask for more info, and whether it targets one specific device.

TypeConnectable?Scannable?Typical use
ADV_INDYesYesStandard: "find me and connect"
ADV_DIRECT_INDYes (1 device)NoFast reconnect to a known central
ADV_SCAN_INDNoYesNot connectable, but can give extra data
ADV_NONCONN_INDNoNoPure beacon (broadcast only)
  • Connectable — a central is allowed to form a connection.
  • Scannable — a central can send a SCAN_REQ to pull a second packet (SCAN_RSP) with extra data (e.g. full device name).
  • Directed — aimed at one specific address, for fast reconnection.
Example

A supermarket beacon uses ADV_NONCONN_IND — it just shouts its ID; nobody connects. A smartwatch uses ADV_IND — discoverable and connectable.

08 Scanning (passive vs active)

Scanning is how a central listens for advertisers. Passive just listens. Active listens, then asks the advertiser for more.

  • Passive scan — only receives adverts. Lower power, less info.
  • Active scan — sends SCAN_REQ, gets a SCAN_RSP back. More data, more power, more air traffic.
  • Scan window / interval — how long you listen vs how often. Window/interval = duty cycle. Bigger window = catch more adverts, burn more power.

09 Connection establishment & parameters

Once a central sends CONNECT_IND, both sides share three numbers that define the whole connection's behavior — the classic power-vs-latency trade-off.

ParameterWhat it meansRange
Connection intervalHow often the two devices talk7.5 ms – 4 s
Peripheral latencyHow many intervals the peripheral may skip if it has nothing to say0 – 499
Supervision timeoutHow long without a packet before declaring the link dead100 ms – 32 s
Example — a wireless mouse vs a temperature sensor

Mouse: tiny interval (7.5 ms), latency 0 → feels instant, uses more power.
Temp sensor: interval 1 s, latency 20 → wakes rarely, sips power, but reacts slowly. Same knobs, opposite tuning.

Gotcha

Peripheral latency lets a device stay responsive to the central while skipping its own wakeups — you get low power without a huge interval. Just keep timeout > interval × (latency+1) or the link drops.

10 Connection events & anchor points

Inside a connection, all traffic happens in short bursts called connection events. Each starts at an anchor point — a precisely scheduled instant, one connection-interval apart. Between events, both radios sleep.

  • Every connection event uses a new data channel (that's the hopping from Topic 04).
  • If either side has more data, it can send multiple packets within one event.
  • Miss the anchor and you burn power / risk timeout — which is why Link Layer timing is hard real-time.
Part 3

GAP — Generic Access Profile

Who's who: roles, discovery, pairing relationships, and addresses.

11 GAP roles

GAP defines four roles. Two are for connections, two are for one-way broadcast.

RoleDoes whatExample
PeripheralAdvertises, accepts a connectionFitness band, sensor
CentralScans, initiates the connectionPhone, gateway
BroadcasterAdvertises only, never connectsBeacon
ObserverScans only, never connectsBeacon reader
Example

Your phone (Central) connects to your watch (Peripheral). Meanwhile a shop's beacon (Broadcaster) shouts an ID that a store display (Observer) is listening for — no connection anywhere in that second pair.

Note

A single chip can play multiple roles at once — e.g. an EV charger that is a Peripheral to a phone app and a Central to onboard sensors.

12 Bonding & filter accept lists

Three related ideas people mix up: pairing, bonding, and the filter accept list.

  • Pairing — exchange keys now to encrypt this session.
  • Bonding — save those keys so next time you reconnect without pairing again. (Pairing + "remember me".)
  • Filter Accept List (formerly "whitelist") — a hardware list of allowed addresses; the controller ignores everyone else. Saves power and blocks strangers.
Example

First time you connect earbuds to your phone: pairing happens. Because they bonded, every future reconnect is instant and silent. The earbuds' filter accept list means they'll only reconnect to your phone.

Terminology

The spec renamed "whitelist → Filter Accept List" and "master/slave → Central/Peripheral." Use the new terms; interviewers notice.

13 Device addresses

Every BLE device has a 48-bit address, but it's not always fixed. Addresses can be random and rotating — that's the basis of privacy.

Address typeBehavior
PublicFixed, factory-assigned (like a MAC). Globally unique, never changes.
Random StaticRandom, but stays the same until reboot/repower.
Resolvable Private (RPA)Rotates periodically; only a bonded device holding the IRK can figure out it's the same device.
Non-resolvable PrivateRotates, and nobody can link the old and new — pure anonymity.
Example

Your phone uses a Resolvable Private Address that changes every ~15 min so trackers can't follow you around a mall — but your paired car, holding the phone's IRK, still recognizes it every time.

Part 4

GATT / ATT — how data is organized

The most important layer for app/firmware developers. Master this.

14 ATT — the Attribute Protocol

Everything in BLE data exchange is an attribute: a small addressable record. GATT is just a set of rules layered on top of these attributes. Each attribute has three parts.

FieldMeaningExample
HandleA unique 16-bit address for this attribute0x0025
Type (UUID)What kind of thing this is0x2A19 (Battery Level)
ValueThe actual data0x5A → 90%
UUIDs

16-bit UUIDs = standard, SIG-defined (Battery, Heart Rate…). 128-bit UUIDs = your own custom services/characteristics. In firmware you'll define custom 128-bit UUIDs for anything proprietary.

Example

The server holds a table of attributes. The client says "read handle 0x0025" and gets back 0x5A. It knows from the UUID that 0x5A means "battery = 90%".

15 GATT hierarchy: Services → Characteristics → Descriptors

GATT organizes attributes into a clean tree. This structure is the interview answer people most want to hear.

  • Profile — a use-case spec (a group of services), not a real attribute.
  • Service — a labeled group of related data (Heart Rate, Battery).
  • Characteristic — one actual data point + its properties (read/write/notify).
  • Descriptor — metadata for a characteristic. The key one is the CCCD — the switch a client flips to turn notifications on.
Remember the CCCD

Client Characteristic Configuration Descriptor. A client writes to the CCCD to subscribe. If notifications "don't work," 90% of the time the client never enabled the CCCD.

16 GATT operations: Read, Write, Notify, Indicate

Four ways data moves. The key split: who initiates, and is it acknowledged.

OperationDirectionAcknowledged?
ReadClient pulls from serverYes
WriteClient pushes to serverYes (Write Request)
Write w/o responseClient pushes to serverNo — faster
NotifyServer pushes to clientNo
IndicateServer pushes to clientYes (client confirms)
Example

Notify: heart-rate monitor streams 72, 73, 74 bpm — losing one sample is fine, so no ack (fast, low power).
Indicate: a glucose meter sends a reading you must not lose — it waits for the client to confirm before sending the next.

Interview one-liner

"Notify = fire-and-forget, Indicate = reliable-but-slower. Both are server-initiated and both require the client to enable the CCCD first."

17 GATT Server vs Client

Roles here are about who holds the data, and they're independent of Central/Peripheral.

  • GATT Server — holds the attribute table (the data). Usually the sensor/peripheral.
  • GATT Client — accesses that data (reads/writes/subscribes). Usually the phone/central.
The trap they'll test

Central ≠ Client. A peripheral can be the GATT client. Example: a sensor (peripheral) reads configuration from the phone (which acts as server). GAP role (who connects) and GATT role (who holds data) are separate axes.

18 MTU negotiation

MTU (Maximum Transmission Unit) = the biggest ATT payload that fits in one exchange. Default is tiny — 23 bytes (so 20 bytes of usable data). Both sides negotiate a larger one at connect time.

Why you care

With the default 20-byte payload, sending 200 bytes takes 10 round trips. Negotiate MTU up to ~247+ and it's one exchange — huge throughput win. Pair MTU with Data Length Extension (Topic 29) for the biggest gains.

Part 5

Security

Pairing, keys, encryption, and privacy — where interviews get sharp.

19 Pairing methods

Pairing establishes trust. Which method is used depends on each device's I/O capabilities (does it have a screen? a keypad?).

MethodHow it worksProtects against MITM?
Just WorksNo user check; keys exchanged silentlyNo
Passkey EntryOne shows a 6-digit code, other types itYes
Numeric ComparisonBoth show a number; user confirms they match (LE SC only)Yes
Out of Band (OOB)Keys shared via another channel (e.g. NFC)Yes (if OOB channel is secure)
Example

A screenless sensor → Just Works. A smart lock with a keypad → Passkey. Two phones → Numeric Comparison ("do both show 458213?"). Tap-to-pair speaker → OOB via NFC.

MITM = Man In The Middle

"Just Works" is convenient but an attacker can silently sit between the two devices. If security matters, you need a method with user interaction.

20 LE Secure Connections vs Legacy Pairing

There are two generations of pairing. Know why the newer one is stronger.

 Legacy Pairing (pre-4.2)LE Secure Connections (4.2+)
Key exchangeCustom, weaker (TK/STK)Elliptic-Curve Diffie-Hellman (ECDH)
EavesdroppingVulnerable if sniffed during pairingSafe even if the pairing is fully sniffed
Numeric ComparisonNot availableAvailable
The key idea

With ECDH, the two devices derive a shared secret without ever sending it over the air. A sniffer sees the whole exchange and still can't compute the key. Always prefer LE Secure Connections.

21 Encryption & the key hierarchy

BLE encrypts the link with AES-128 CCM. Pairing produces a set of keys, each with a job.

KeyFull nameJob
LTKLong Term KeyEncrypts the link (and reused on every reconnect after bonding)
IRKIdentity Resolving KeyResolves a rotating private address back to one identity
CSRKConnection Signature Resolving KeySigns data for authenticity without full encryption
Example

After bonding, your car stores your phone's LTK (to re-encrypt instantly) and IRK (to recognize the phone even though its address keeps rotating). No re-pairing, ever.

22 Privacy & address resolution

Privacy = stopping strangers from tracking a device by its address. BLE solves it by rotating a Resolvable Private Address (RPA) that only trusted peers can un-rotate.

Address resolution

A bonded peer stores your IRK. When it sees an RPA, it runs the resolution math; if it matches, it knows it's you. Everyone else sees an address that changes every ~15 min — untrackable.

Part 6

HCI & Stack Integration

How the software actually sits on real chips.

23 HCI — commands, events, transport

HCI is the standardized language between Host and Controller. The Host sends commands; the Controller sends back events and data. Because it's standardized, you can mix a host stack from one vendor with a controller from another.

  • Commands — Host → Controller ("start advertising", "connect to X").
  • Events — Controller → Host ("connection complete", "data received").
  • ACL data — the actual payload packets in both directions.
  • Transport — the physical wire carrying HCI: UART (most common in embedded), USB, SPI.
Example

A Linux board (host, running BlueZ) talks over UART to a BLE controller chip. Your firmware or BlueZ issues HCI commands; the chip does the radio timing and reports events back. A BLE sniffer that captures HCI shows you exactly this command/event traffic.

24 SoC vs dual-chip (Host-Controller) architectures

Two ways to physically build a BLE product. This is a real design-decision question.

 SoCDual-chip
Everything onOne chip (nRF52, ESP32)Host MCU + separate BLE controller
Cost/sizeCheaper, smallerMore parts
WhenSimple standalone devicesExisting MCU/CPU needs BLE bolted on (e.g. a Linux SoC + BLE module)
Your context

An i.MX93 running Linux is the "host" side and would use a separate BLE controller/module over HCI — classic dual-chip. An nRF52 or ESP32 is a single SoC where your firmware and the stack share one chip.

25 Stack vendors & platforms

You rarely write a BLE stack from scratch — you build on a vendor's. Knowing the main ones signals you've shipped real products.

Stack / platformRuns on
Nordic SoftDevice / nRF Connect SDK (Zephyr)nRF52 / nRF53 — the industry favorite
ESP-IDF (Bluedroid / NimBLE)ESP32 family
Zephyr RTOS BLEVendor-neutral, many MCUs

On Linux boards

On a Pi or i.MX93 you'll drive BLE through BlueZ — via its D-Bus API (Python bluezero/dbus) or command-line bluetoothctl / btmgmt / hcitool. That's the practical entry point for your projects below.

Part 7

Firmware & Practical Engineering

The day-job skills: power, event handling, updates, throughput, debugging.

26 Power optimization

The whole point of BLE is battery life. As firmware engineer, power tuning is your core job.

  • Sleep aggressively — the radio and MCU should be in deep sleep between connection/advertising events. Idle current in µA, not mA.
  • Longer connection interval — fewer wakeups. Balance against responsiveness.
  • Peripheral latency — skip empty wakeups while staying reachable.
  • Slower advertising interval — advertising every 1000 ms vs 20 ms is a huge power difference (but slower to discover).
  • Minimize TX power & payload — send less, less often, at the lowest power that reaches.
  • Use 2M PHY — same data, less time with the radio on.
Example

A sensor advertising every 20 ms might last days. Move to 1000 ms advertising + a 500 ms connection interval + deep sleep between, and the same coin cell lasts a year or more.

27 Event-driven firmware

BLE stacks are asynchronous and event-driven. You don't poll — you register callbacks and react. This shapes how you write the whole firmware.

  • The stack raises events: connected, disconnected, data written, MTU updated, notification sent.
  • You handle each in a callback and return fast — never block in a BLE callback (it runs in a time-critical context).
  • Heavy work goes to a work queue / RTOS task, not the callback itself.
  • On an RTOS (Zephyr, FreeRTOS) the stack often runs in its own high-priority thread; you talk to it via queues.
Interview gold

Say: "I treat BLE callbacks like ISRs — do the minimum, defer the rest to a work queue. Blocking in a GATT write handler can break connection timing." That answer maps your RTOS/Cortex-M background straight onto BLE.

28 OTA / DFU — firmware update over BLE

DFU (Device Firmware Update) pushes a new firmware image to a device wirelessly. Essential for any deployed product.

  • Dual-bank (A/B) — write the new image to a spare slot; only swap after it's verified. Bad image → roll back. Safe.
  • Verify before boot — CRC + cryptographic signature so you don't run tampered/corrupt firmware.
  • Speed it up — 2M PHY + big MTU + Data Length Extension make OTA far faster.
  • Nordic has a mature DFU; MCUboot is the common bootloader in Zephyr.
Your EV-charger angle

OTA is directly relevant to charging products — updating deployed field units without a technician. Framing your firmware experience around "secure, resumable, rollback-safe updates" lands well.

29 Throughput optimization

Default BLE throughput is low. Real applications tune four knobs together.

KnobEffect
2M PHYDoubles raw data rate
Larger MTUMore data per packet, fewer round trips
Data Length Extension (DLE)Bigger link-layer packets (up to 251 bytes payload)
Shorter connection intervalMore events/sec (costs power)
Example

Default ≈ a few kbps effective. Turn on 2M PHY + MTU 247 + DLE + a tight interval and BLE 5 can push toward ~1.3 Mbps effective. That's the difference between a firmware update taking minutes vs seconds.

Say this

"MTU and DLE work together — a big MTU is wasted if the link-layer packet is still 27 bytes. You raise both, plus 2M PHY, to actually see the throughput."

30 Debugging tools & sniffers

You can't debug radio by staring at code. BLE work leans hard on sniffers and logging.

  • Over-the-air sniffers — nRF Sniffer (nRF52 dongle + Wireshark, free) and Ellisys/Frontline (pro). Capture actual packets on air.
  • HCI logging — on Linux, btmon shows every HCI command/event. On phones, enable the "Bluetooth HCI snoop log".
  • Phone scanner apps — nRF Connect app to inspect services/characteristics, subscribe, read/write live.
  • RTT / UART logs — Segger RTT for low-overhead firmware logs that don't disturb timing.
  • Power profiler — Nordic PPK2 to see current draw per event and validate sleep.
Typical bug hunt

"Notifications not arriving." Sniff the air → see the client never wrote the CCCD. Fixed in 5 minutes. Without the sniffer you'd guess for hours.

Part 8

Advanced & Newer Features

Bluetooth 5.x and beyond — good to mention, shows current awareness.

31 Bluetooth 5.x features

Bluetooth 5 (2016) was a big jump for BLE. Know the headline four.

  • 2M PHY — 2× throughput.
  • Coded PHY (Long Range) — up to ~4× range.
  • Advertising Extensions — much bigger advertising payloads on the data channels, more beacon capacity.
  • Periodic Advertising — scheduled, connectionless broadcasts multiple listeners can sync to (foundation of Auracast).
Later 5.x

5.1 added Direction Finding (AoA/AoD) for indoor positioning. 5.2 added LE Audio + Isochronous Channels (Topic 32). 5.3/5.4 refined power and added PAwR for electronic shelf labels.

32 LE Audio & Isochronous channels

LE Audio moves audio onto the low-energy radio (Classic used to own audio). It runs over new Isochronous Channels (ISO) — time-sensitive streams with guaranteed timing.

  • LC3 codec — better quality at lower bitrate than Classic's SBC.
  • CIS (Connected Isochronous Stream) — point-to-point, e.g. true-wireless earbuds with synced L/R.
  • BIS (Broadcast Isochronous Stream) — one source, unlimited listeners.
  • Auracast — broadcast audio to any nearby receiver (airport gate announcements, silent gym TVs).
Example

A gym TV broadcasts audio via Auracast/BIS; anyone's earbuds tune in. Your earbuds' left/right sync over CIS. Both are ISO, both are LE Audio.

33 Bluetooth Mesh

Mesh is a separate networking layer on top of BLE advertising for many-to-many device networks — think a whole building of smart lights.

  • Managed flooding — messages hop node-to-node to cover large areas; no central hub needed.
  • Relay nodes — rebroadcast messages to extend reach.
  • Publish/subscribe model with group addresses (all "kitchen" lights).
  • It rides on advertising, not connections — very different mental model from GATT.
Example

200 smart bulbs across an office. Flip one switch → the message floods through relay bulbs to every light tagged "floor-2," even ones out of direct range of the switch.

34 Coexistence with Wi-Fi & other 2.4 GHz radios

BLE, Wi-Fi, Zigbee, and often BLE+Wi-Fi on the same chip all fight over 2.4 GHz. Coexistence is how they share without killing each other.

  • AFH (Topic 04) — BLE's built-in defense: avoid busy channels.
  • PTA (Packet Traffic Arbitration) — on combo chips, a hardware arbiter decides which radio transmits when, so BLE and Wi-Fi don't talk over each other.
  • Channel planning — keep BLE advertising channels away from your Wi-Fi channel.
  • Time-slicing — schedule BLE and Wi-Fi activity into separate time windows.
Your context

An i.MX93 or ESP32 gateway often runs Wi-Fi + BLE together — coexistence is a real, testable design concern, not trivia. PTA on combo modules is the usual answer.
